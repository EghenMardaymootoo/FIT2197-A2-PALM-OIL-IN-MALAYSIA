A data story

Palm Oil in Malaysia

Growth, forests, and the decoupling.

By Eghen Mardaymootoo · Monash University Malaysia · 2026

1. The world runs on it

Palm oil is in about half of all packaged consumer goods on a supermarket shelf. Two countries grow almost all of it and Malaysia is one of them.

In 1970, Malaysia produced less than half a million tonnes of crude palm oil. Today, it produces more than forty times that. The growth was not gradual — it accelerated through three distinct policy eras.

One reason the world cannot simply abandon palm oil: no other oil crop comes close on yield. Replacing palm with soy or rapeseed would require many times more land — and almost certainly, more cleared forest somewhere else.

2. Where palm oil lives in Malaysia

Roughly two-thirds of Malaysia's agricultural land is under oil palm. But the crop is not spread evenly — and neither is the forest loss that followed it.

The two maps almost rhyme. The states with the most palm oil are the states that have lost the most forest. But this is only the cumulative picture — it tells you nothing about whether things are still getting worse, or whether something has changed.

3. Something changed in 2016

For four decades, palm oil production and forest loss rose together. Then, around 2016, the two lines split.

But the national average hides a more uneven story. Some states genuinely greened. Others kept clearing. Find your state below.

Sabah and Johor have done the hardest thing in the data: grown the industry while cutting less forest. Kelantan and Pahang are still on the older trajectory.

4. Who is pushing on the industry

Decoupling doesn't happen by accident. It happens because someone is pushing — buyers, regulators, and the industry's own certification bodies.

Malaysia has been quietly moving up the value chain. Less raw crude palm oil leaves the country today; more leaves as refined products, oleochemicals, and biodiesel.

5. The story isn't over

The decoupling is real. It is also unfinished. Two states accounted for most of Malaysia's primary forest loss in 2024.

For the average Malaysian, palm oil is a bottle on a kitchen shelf. For the country, it is a balance between an industry that feeds millions and a forest that cannot regrow on demand. The decoupling shows the balance is not impossible. The states still clearing show the work is not done.
